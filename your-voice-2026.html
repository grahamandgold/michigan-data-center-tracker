Why this matters

Every chair in the Capitol is up for grabs

All 110 House seats and all 38 Senate seats are on the ballot in 2026 — the primary is Aug 4 and the general is Nov 3. Whoever wins the most chairs controls the Capitol — and sets the rules for data centers: power lines, energy costs, water use, noise limits, and tax breaks.

How we built this
Methodology
A nonpartisan record of where data centers are proposed in Michigan and what the people on your 2026 ballot have said about them. Here is exactly how it is put together.
The voter guide

We track what 2026 candidates for Governor, U.S. Senate, and the state Legislature have said, on the record, about data centers. Every entry links to a public source — news coverage, a debate, a campaign statement, or an official post. We quote candidates and cite the source; we do not rate, rank, or endorse.

The district map

Each Michigan House and Senate seat is shaded by the number of active and proposed data-center projects inside its boundaries, drawn from our project tracker. Withdrawn, rejected, and cancelled proposals are excluded, so the map reflects live development pressure — not every idea ever floated. Megawatts shown on hover are the developer’s disclosed capacity; many have disclosed none.

District boundaries

Michigan House districts use the 2024 court-ordered remedial plan; Senate districts use the 2021 Independent Citizens Redistricting Commission plan — both published by the State of Michigan GIS. Each project is matched to its district by mapped coordinates (point-in-polygon).

Corrections

This is a living record and we will sometimes get things wrong. If a quote, source, project, or district looks off, tell us and we will fix it — corrections are logged publicly. Corrections policy →
